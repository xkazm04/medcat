# The lack of pricing information is concerning. Please change research strategy to look at national registries with vendors and public offers across EU countries. Target would be to find these registries where you can access this valuable information and extract

National implant price data in the EU is usually embedded in reimbursement and procurement systems rather than a single “price registry,” so the realistic strategy is to mine a mix of: (1) national reimbursement lists where implant brands are coded and sometimes priced, and (2) public procurement portals where hospitals publish tender unit prices for primary/revision knee prostheses.[^2_1][^2_2][^2_3]

## 1. Countries with brand‑level device lists or tariffs

These are priority targets if you want systematic, reusable data:

- **France – LPPR (Liste des Produits et Prestations Remboursables)**
    - Central list for reimbursable medical devices, including joint prostheses; some entries are **brand‑specific** and include negotiated prices or tariff ceilings.[^2_2][^2_1]
    - Access via the French health insurance or HAS portals; search by product family and manufacturer, then filter to knee revision prostheses.
- **Germany – DRG + implant add‑on payments**
    - Base payment is DRG‑based, but **joint replacement materials can attract specific add‑on reimbursement**, sometimes with class‑level or brand‑level codes.[^2_2]
    - Tools like reimbursement.info expose OPS codes and DRG/add‑on payment for particular procedures (e.g., joint surgery codes), but full tariff details require registration or country‑specific portals.[^2_4]
- **Italy – National DRG tariff list**
    - National DRG 545 “Revision of hip or knee replacement” has a published national tariff (e.g. €11,152), which gives a ceiling for the whole revision episode, not per implant.[^2_2]
    - No central public implant price list, but regional procurement consortia sometimes publish tender unit prices.
- **Spain – Common Package of Benefits + regional procurement**
    - Implants are funded via DRG plus inclusion in the Common Package of Benefits; price negotiations happen at national/ regional level.[^2_1][^2_2]
    - Real per‑implant prices appear in regional procurement portals (e.g. autonomous community tender platforms).
- **Nordic and other registries**
    - There are 24 hip and knee arthroplasty registries in Europe, covering ~2.5 million knee arthroplasties, but they primarily track volume, not price.[^2_3]
    - Some national health services (e.g. NHS England/Scotland) set **ceiling prices** for hip/knee implant lots in framework agreements; unit or ceiling prices appear in tender documents.[^2_5]


## 2. Public procurement / hospital tender sources

For actual **line‑item prices per implant family** (often including revision components), the best EU‑level sources are:

- **TED / Publications Office of the EU**
    - The EU’s Tenders Electronic Daily contains framework agreements and contracts for “primary and revision knee implants” with price forms as annexes.[^2_6][^2_7]
    - Example: an EU procedure explicitly for “Primary and revision knee implants”; detailed price forms (with unit prices per lot) are in attachments referenced in the notice.[^2_6]
    - Strategy: search CPV 33183100 (orthopaedic implants) with keywords “revision knee”, then pull annexes/price forms.
- **National procurement portals (examples)**
    - **Poland** – local hospital tenders for “knee joint endoprostheses” publish a “Price Form (OPZ)” as annex where bidders fill in unit prices for each component (including revision lots).[^2_8]
    - **UK / NHS (not EU but useful benchmark)** – NHS Scotland tender for “Orthopaedic Hip \& Knee Implants \& Consumables” defines lots for revision knee and sometimes ceiling prices per implant lot.[^2_5]
    - Similar structures exist in Italy, Spain, Czech Republic, etc., but access and machine‑readability vary; many are in national languages.

In practice, these annexes are PDFs/Excels where each line is “Manufacturer – Product group – Unit price,” and revision femoral/tibial components are typically split into separate items.

## 3. Why registries rarely give implant‑level prices

- Arthroplasty registries (e.g. in Scandinavia, UK, Germany) focus on **implant survival, revision rates, and outcomes**, not commercial terms.[^2_3]
- Economic studies of knee revision in Europe show **episode‑level reimbursement** (e.g. €11–23k per revision in Germany/Ireland/France, up to ~€60k in Spain/Switzerland), but they derive costs from DRG reimbursements and hospital accounting, not public implant pricelists.[^2_9]
- Dedicated reimbursement/market‑access services (e.g. MTRC) build private databases by combining DRG tariffs, add‑on payments, and LPPR codes, but this data is paywalled.[^2_10][^2_2]

So for **implant SKU‑level prices** (e.g. specific femoral revision cones), procurement documents and occasional hospital price lists are realistically your only public sources.

2. **Automate TED / national portal scraping for CPV 33183100 \& keywords**
